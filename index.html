K-12 Education and Technology

Industry background

K-12 schools across the world seek to provide and deliver top-tier educational services to students across the globe, providing a rich teaching a learning curriculum.

Over the years, K-12 institutions have adopted cutting edge technologies to enhance the services they provide, giving them the competitive advantage over their counterparts. Students, faculty and staff members are equipped with technological devices to connect and access school resources.




These schools have different IT infrastructure models to support teaching and learning, that ranges from:

  • Online enrolment systems to manage studen
  • Admissions and acceptance, student information system for the management of academic and demographic data for the duration of the student at the school
  • Learning management systems to deliver educational content
  • Library management systems and many more.
Research indicates that the COVID-19 pandemic forced most of these schools to deliver distant learning for the 2020-2021 academic year (Anne Keeling, 2020). Due to the vast injection of technology in the K-12 setting, we have decided to dive deeper to see where we can use AI for cyber security to add value.

Threat trends

The education industry faces cyber threats from following actors:

  • Advanced Persistent Threat (APT) groups attempting to gain access to sensitive intellectual property, such as from university research centers, for economic or political espionage.
  • APT groups aiming to use an educational institution’s network infrastructure as a staging ground from which to target victims in other industries, on the assumption that their activity will appear less suspicious originating from a reputable institution’s network.
  • Enterprise-like cybercriminals seeking to steal and profit from sensitive personal and financial information from students, faculty, and staff
  • Hacktivists trying to deface and disrupt websites as a method of protest or way to call attention to a certain cause.

Threat Modelling

Critical Asset Identification

Asset 1

Student and Staff Data

  • From 2016 to 2020, significant number of K-12 students has the personal information compromised with data breaches.
  • The types of data compromised ranged from grades, bullying reports, to Social Security numbers. This makes students vulnerable to emotional, physical, and financial trauma.

Asset 2

Financial and credit identity

  • Reported breaches happened more in wealthy and larger suburban school districts.
  • Denials for credit cards and car loans revealed a lot of these incidents.
  • The value of assets compromised ranged from 0.2 million to 9.8 million US dollars, due to school officials leaking banking information of the school mistakenly.

Asset 3

Education availability and quality learning time

  • Manpower and IT investments have been spent to provide quality education online despite the pandemic.
  • Disruption of classes and closure of schools resulted in wastage of these investment, as well as a lack of education provision to affected students.

Asset 4

Our children’s safety and wellness

The safety and wellness of young children are at risks with racist, sexist and anti-Semitic hate speech, violence threats and pornography such as live sex acts.

Intelligence buy-in

Schools are valuable because of the knowledge they can provide to hackersbecause of the data they can provide.That data not just student grades, but student and employee Social Securitynumbers, employee bank account numbers, and other personally identifiableinformation makes educational institutions the third most frequent target ofhackers, just after healthcare and financial services.

AI is fast emerging as a must-have technology for enhancing the performance of IT security. Humans can no longer scale to sufficiently secure an enterprise-level attack surface, and AI gives the much-needed analysis and threat identification that can be used by security professionals to minimize breach risk and enhance security posture.
Moreover, AI can help discover and prioritize risks, direct incident response, and identify malware attacks before they come into the picture.
Organizations can dramatically reduce losses arising from data breaches and Cyber Threats by comprehensively automating their response and containment capabilities.
